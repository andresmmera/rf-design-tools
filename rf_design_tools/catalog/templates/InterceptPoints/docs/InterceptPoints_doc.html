Intercept Point

IPn Tool

The intercept point, IPn = (IIPn, OIPn), is the point in the {Pin, Pout} plane where the ideal fundamental output power (Pout) intersects with the output power of the intermodulation product of order n (IMn). In this sense, the IPn quantifies the linearity of the system against intermodulation distortion of order n, so, the higher IP, the higher system linearity.

It is important to note that the intercept point cannot be achieved in a real experiment since the output power suffers from AM/AM distortion (compression) and it becomes saturated at some high drive level.

This is a linear method for solving a nonlinear problem, but it is a simple and intuitive tool, and it is widely used for designing nonlinear RF circuits, since it allows us to predict the level of the IM products.

The power of the n-th intermodulation product at the output of the nonlinear device is given by:

$$ P_{out, IMn} = {n·P_{in}} - { (n-1)·IIP_{n} } + G \tag{1} $$

and, obviously, the fundamental output power is related to the input as:

$$ P_{out} = { P_{in} + G} \tag{2} $$

The IPn can be estimated from the two-tone test as [1]:

$$OIP_{n} = P_{out} + {\Delta \over {n-1} } \tag{3}$$

$$IIP_{n} = P_{out} - G + {\Delta \over {n-1} } \tag{4}$$

where Pout is the power of the carrier, G is the (linear) gain of the amplifier and Δ is the level (in dB) between Pout and the power level of the intermodulation products of n order. Notice that OIPn = IIPn + G

Compression

The compression point indicates the output power at which the actual output power coming from the device deviates n dBs (typically 1, but also 3 or 5dBs) from its ideal linear gain. As an amplifier enters in the saturation region, the actual gain is reduced because of its AM/AM nonlinear behavior. It is important to note that this kind of nonlinerity is not related to the intermodulated distortion. Also notice that the IP3 is a fictitious point, but the P1dB can be measured with a network analyzer doing a power sweep.
